If you're presenting

Low prep, high signal. Here's how to make the most of your 60 minutes.

  1. Don't make a deck — Screen-share your Figma, prototype, or working doc. Messy is fine; real is better than polished.
  2. Start with the "why" — Two sentences on the user problem and business context. Not everyone knows your project.
  3. Name your stage — Early exploration? Final polish? This calibrates the feedback you'll receive.
  4. Say what feedback you want — "Does this flow make sense?" beats "any thoughts?" Be specific about where you need eyes.
  5. Show the hard decisions — The forks in the road are the most interesting part. Share what you chose and why.
  6. It's a conversation, not a keynote — Expect interruptions. That's a signal people are engaged.
5 / 8

If you're in the audience

Great feedback is a skill. Here's how to make yours count.

  1. Listen for the stage — Early work needs directional feedback, not pixel-level critique.
  2. Lead with questions, not opinions — "What did you consider for X?" opens more doors than "I think you should do Y."
  3. Connect, don't compete — If their problem overlaps yours, say so. That's the whole point of this session.
  4. Be specific — "The hierarchy feels unclear on the second screen" beats "looks good."
  5. Follow up after — If you have a deeper thought, DM the presenter. The time window is tight.

Match feedback to the stage

The kind of feedback that helps depends on where the work is.

Early / Exploring
Divergent
  • Is this the right problem to solve?
  • Are there approaches you haven't considered?
  • Who else should you talk to?
  • What assumptions are you making?
Mid-fidelity
Convergent
  • Does the flow make sense end-to-end?
  • What's confusing or surprising?
  • Does this scale to edge cases?
  • Are the trade-offs well reasoned?
Late / Shipping
Polishing
  • Are the details and micro-interactions polished?
  • Anything inconsistent with existing patterns?
  • Edge cases or error states missing?
  • Is the handoff spec clear for engineering?
